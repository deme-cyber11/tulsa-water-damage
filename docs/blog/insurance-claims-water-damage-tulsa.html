Insurance Guide

How to File a Water Damage Insurance Claim in Oklahoma

January 2025 · 8 min read · By Tulsa Water Damage Pros

Water damage is one of the most common homeowner's insurance claims in Oklahoma — and one of the most confusing to navigate. Between determining what's covered, working with adjusters, and understanding what a "reasonable and customary" repair actually means, the process can be overwhelming when you're already dealing with the stress of a flooded home.

We've filed hundreds of water damage claims alongside Tulsa homeowners. Here's what we've learned.

Step 1: Determine If You Have Coverage

Not all water damage is covered by standard Oklahoma homeowner's insurance. Understanding the distinction before you call your insurer prevents surprises later:

  • Covered: Sudden and accidental water damage — burst pipes, appliance failures, ice dam damage, wind-driven rain entering through storm damage
  • Usually covered with endorsement: Water and sewer backup — not always standard, but many Oklahoma policies include it
  • Not covered by standard policy: Rising floodwater from outside your home (requires NFIP flood policy), damage from gradual leaks or deferred maintenance

If you're unsure whether your specific situation is covered, call us at (918) 723-2096 before calling your insurer. We can help you understand what's likely covered based on the cause of the damage.

Step 2: Document Extensively Before Restoration Begins

This is the step most homeowners skip — and it's the one that costs them the most on their claim. Before any restoration work begins, document everything:

  • Photograph and video every affected room from multiple angles
  • Capture close-ups of damaged materials — saturated drywall, warped flooring, wet insulation
  • Document damaged contents — furniture, electronics, clothing, appliances
  • Note the date and time of discovery and the apparent cause
  • Take photos of the water source (broken pipe, storm entry point, etc.)

When we arrive, we also create comprehensive documentation including moisture readings, thermal imagery, and detailed damage reports — all in formats that insurance adjusters recognize and accept.

Step 3: File Your Claim Promptly

Most Tulsa-area insurers have 24/7 claim reporting:

  • State Farm: 1-800-STATEFARM (800-782-8332)
  • Farmers: 1-800-435-7764
  • Allstate: 1-800-ALLSTATE (800-255-7828)
  • USAA: 1-800-531-8722
  • Liberty Mutual: 1-800-225-2467

When filing, you'll need: your policy number, the date of loss, a description of what happened and what was damaged, and your contact information. Get a claim number — this is how you'll track the claim going forward.

Step 4: Understand the Adjuster's Role

Your insurance company will send an adjuster to inspect the damage. Adjusters work for the insurance company — their job is to assess the claim fairly, but their employer's interest is in paying out as little as justifiable. This isn't nefarious, but it means you should be prepared:

  • Be present during the adjuster's visit if at all possible
  • Have your damage documentation ready to share
  • If we're already on-site, our certified technicians can walk the adjuster through the damage assessment
  • Do not sign any releases or settlements until you understand what's included
  • You have the right to request a re-inspection or hire a public adjuster if you believe the initial assessment is too low

Step 5: Work With Your Restoration Company on Documentation

A quality IICRC-certified restoration company will produce documentation that makes your adjuster's job easier and your claim stronger. At Tulsa Water Damage Pros, we provide:

  • Written scope of work in Xactimate format (the industry-standard estimating software all major insurers use)
  • Daily moisture logs documenting drying progress
  • Material identification for removed items (what type of flooring, drywall, insulation was affected)
  • Photo documentation at each stage of the restoration process

We can communicate directly with your adjuster if you authorize it, which often speeds up approval and reduces disputes about what needs to be replaced vs. repaired.

Step 6: Understand Your Deductible and ACV vs. RCV

Two terms that trip up many Oklahoma homeowners:

Deductible: The amount you pay before insurance kicks in. Oklahoma homeowner's policies typically have a standard deductible of $500–$2,500, plus potentially a separate, higher deductible for wind and hail damage. Know your deductible before you file — if the damage is close to that amount, it may not make sense to file (claims affect your premium).

ACV vs. RCV: Actual Cash Value pays you what your damaged items are worth today (depreciated value). Replacement Cost Value pays what it costs to replace them new. RCV policies are more expensive but pay significantly more for older homes with older materials. Check which you have.
