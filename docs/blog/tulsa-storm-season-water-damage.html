Preparing Your Tulsa Home for Oklahoma Storm Season

January 2025 · 7 min read · By Tulsa Water Damage Pros

Living in Green Country means living with severe weather. Tulsa and the surrounding metro area — Broken Arrow, Owasso, Jenks, Sand Springs — sit in one of the most active storm corridors in the United States. Every spring, supercell thunderstorms march across eastern Oklahoma, bringing tornado warnings, baseball-sized hail, and rainfall events that can dump 3–5 inches in a matter of hours.

The good news: most storm-related water damage is preventable with the right preparation. Here's what Tulsa homeowners should do before storm season arrives.

Understand Your Flood Risk

Tulsa has invested heavily in flood control infrastructure since the devastating 1984 flood along the Arkansas River, but many neighborhoods still have meaningful flood risk. FEMA's flood maps are available at msc.fema.gov and show whether your property is in a Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA). If you are, consider adding National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) coverage to your standard homeowner's policy — this is separate coverage that most people don't realize they need.

Inspect Your Roof Before Spring

Oklahoma hail storms are brutal on roofing materials. A roof with existing damage — even just a few cracked shingles from last year's hail — can become a major water intrusion point during the next severe storm. Have your roof inspected by a licensed roofing contractor in February or early March, before storm season begins. Look for:

  • Missing or cracked shingles
  • Damaged flashing around chimneys, vents, and skylights
  • Clogged or damaged gutters that can cause water to back up under the roofline
  • Soft spots on the roof deck indicating previous water damage

Clear Your Gutters and Downspouts

Tulsa's trees — particularly the post oaks, elms, and pecans common in older neighborhoods like Midtown — drop enormous amounts of debris. Clogged gutters during a heavy storm cause water to overflow and pool against your foundation, which is one of the leading causes of basement and crawl space flooding. Clean gutters twice a year: fall after leaf drop and late winter before storm season.

Ensure your downspouts direct water at least 6 feet away from your foundation. Consider extensions if they're currently terminating right at the foundation perimeter.

Test Your Sump Pump

If your Tulsa home has a basement or crawl space with a sump pump, test it before storm season by slowly pouring water into the sump pit until the float rises and triggers the pump. If it doesn't activate, replace it before the spring rains arrive. Consider a battery-backup sump pump for long power outages — which are common during severe Oklahoma storm events.

Know Where Your Water Main Shutoff Is

In a water damage emergency, knowing where your main shutoff is located can prevent thousands of dollars in additional damage. Walk through your home now and locate it. The main shutoff in most Tulsa homes is either in the utility room, crawl space, or near where the main water line enters the home from the street.

Review Your Insurance Coverage

Before storm season, review your homeowner's policy with your agent — whether you're with State Farm, Farmers, Allstate, USAA, or Oklahoma Farm Bureau. Understand what's covered:

  • Typically covered: Wind-driven rain through roof or window damage, water from interior plumbing failures, appliance malfunctions
  • Typically NOT covered: Flooding from rising groundwater (requires NFIP flood policy), sewer backup (may require endorsement), gradual leaks from lack of maintenance

Consider adding a water backup and sewer endorsement to your policy — it's typically inexpensive and covers one of the most common and expensive water damage scenarios in older Tulsa neighborhoods.
